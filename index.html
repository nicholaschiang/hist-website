Creators of the Net.
How the world wide web came to be.
Vint Cerf

Alongside Bob Kahn, Vint Cerf co-founded ISOC (the Internet Society) and worked to develop modern internet protocols including the IP/TCP protocols that your computer used to reach this site.

Graduating from Stanford with a B.S. and later attending UCLA for his M.S. and PhD, Vint Cerf is pretty much genius. While still in high school, Vint worked on the Apollo program before moving on to pursue a career at DARPA (the US Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency that sponsors giant robot races.)

Bob Kahn

Also a DARPA veteran, Bob Kahn developed was the first to demonstrate the real-world capabilities of packet-switching (the system that now powers the reliability of the modern web) through his development of the ARPANET for the ICCC.

After developing the IP/TCP protocols during his time at DARPA, Bob spearheaded the billion dollar US Strategic Computing Initiative and later went on to found the CNRI (Corporation for National Research Initiatives) of which he is currently CEO and president.

Tim Berners-Lee

Building off previous work on IPs, Tim Berners-Lee is best known for the development of the world wide web (that WWW that you see in front of modern URLs).

Tim is primarily responsible for the development of modern URLs, HTML (the language that built the website you're looking at right now), and HTTP (hypertext transfer protocol which sends HTML around).

Evolution.
How we came to use the internet everyday.
1960
US Government ARPANET

The founding father of internet protocol, the ARPANET (the Advanced Research Projects Network) was a project funded by the US Department of Defense.
Building off previous work in packet switching (i.e. a reliable way to send information from one location to another), the ARPANET developed a communication network that would later become the basis for the first computer networks.01

30 1969 AUG
Design of Internet Protocal

With the help of government funding, Bob Khan and Vint Cerf designed the Internet Protocals (IPv4) and Transmission Control Protocals (TCPv4) that enable you to watch cat videos late at night.
Right now, we are actually in a world-wide transition from IPv4 to IPv6 to accomodate the unprecendented growth in Internet-enabled devices. However, most of us still use IPv4 (you're probably using it right now) due to another ingenius protocol powered by LANs.02

1 1983 JAN
Creation of the World Wide Web

Building off of work at the US government's DARPA (creators of the ARPANET), Tim Berners-Lee developed the World Wide Web: originally a small network of university computers for CERN (a nuclear research initiative). He created modern HTML, HTTP, and (most importantly) the URLs that you use everyday to visit your favorite sites (like this one).03

1987 APR
The First Web Browser

The creation of Mosaic (the first cross-platform web browser to gain attention from "non-geeks") greatly helped spread use and knowledge of the web across the world.
Marc Andreessen and his team at the NCSA (the National Center for Supercomputer Applications) worked to make the Internet accessible to the average Joe through their browser's GUI (graphical user interface).04

23 1993 JAN
The Household Web

After being widely adopted for academic and business purposes, the world wide web quickly made its way into the life of the average consumer. In 1995, millions of new home users obtained access to the web when the first ISPs (Compuserve, American Online, and Prodigy) began providing gateways to the Internet. 05

Corruption.
 How the internet became a business.
Reliability and Redundancy

When you ask for www.google.com from your web browser, you usually (I hope) don't worry about not being able to connect. It always works (well, besides that one time where you didn't have WiFi turned on). You don't have to think about it. And you shouldn't.

The Internet was designed in such a way that all packets (the data that gets sent between your computer and Google's servers) should make their way to their destination, even if the power in Iraq is down or the IXP that you normally connect to is under maintenance.

TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) routes packets systematically through the quickest avaliable routes, instead of using preset routes that could become compromised. Thus, the more routers/pathways that are added to the system, the more reliable the Internet becomes. But this reliability comes at a cost.

Internet Service Providers

Imagine that you’re in Los Angeles, and want to take a sip of the Colorado River watershed from your kitchen faucet for free, without paying an existing water utility. How would you do it? You’d need to construct your own ‘private line’ aqueduct from your house to a tributary, and somehow avoid prosecution for siphoning off a controlled resource. Otherwise, you’d need access to pumping stations, valves, existing aqueducts, dams, etc. In short, you’d need to become your own private water utility, whether you interconnect with other existing water utilities or not.

Because people can’t see or touch IP packets, they seem to think their delivery is just an abstraction. It’s not. Internet access requires real physical infrastructure just like gas, water, electricity, and sewage. Protocols layered on top of that infrastructure still require the underlying infrastructure for delivery.

Internet service providers maintain and upgrade that infrastructure, ensuring that you'll always be able to access www.google.com in a timely manner.

Infrastructure and Utility

The Internet is sort of like any other utility. To connect, you pay your ISP (e.g. AT&T, XFinity) for certain "internet speeds" every month. But the Internet is just a bunch of inter-connected computers, right? Why do I need to pay to talk with other computers? Lets say you really didn't want to pay for Internet. You would have to, in a sense, wire your computer directly to an IXP (something that isn't going to happen anytime soon). It's almost like you didn't want to pay for water.

Net Neutrality

But ISPs, like any other business, have to make money. And while they definitely do take their cut from your utility bills, they are being paid for much more. Whenever you try to reference a URL or site that doesn't point anymore, ISPs jump in and use that oppurtunity to show you "related content". Content that they are paid to show you.

Recently, there have been actions regarding "net neutrality" laws. Such laws would, in theory, protect the rights of individuals on the net and ensure that no one individual or company (e.g. ISPs) has power over what can be accessed on the web.
